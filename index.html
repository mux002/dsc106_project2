Project 2 Persuasive or Deceptive Visualization?

Yiming Han (yih059@ucsd.edu), Tiantian Zheng (t3zheng@ucsd.edu), Ziling Xu (zix021@ucsd.edu), Jenny Xu (mux002@ucsd.edu)

Proposition: In primary and secondary education, gender Equality has been achieved.

FOR the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • Used a histogram to emphasize the overall distribution of GPI values rather than individual country differences, creating a sense of global consistency.
  • Included a vertical reference line at GPI = 1.0 to visually reinforce the idea of gender equality being achieved.
  • By omitting country labels and focusing on the majority cluster, the visualization subtly downplays remaining disparities and strengthens the perception of equality.

AGAINST the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • Applied selective filtering to only include outlier countries, exaggerating inequality by removing balanced examples.
  • Labeled each country and its GPI value, giving disproportionate attention to exceptions and evoking concern about inequality.
  • Added a reference line at GPI = 1.0 to draw immediate attention to deviations, even when they are relatively minor.

Final Reflection

Across the two views, we used opposite techniques to see how framing changes what people take away. In the FOR chart, we used a histogram and a reference line at GPI = 1.0. Creating the visualization was fairly straightforward, and we consider it an earnest approach. The bins are clear, 1.0 is the right baseline for parity, and the chart summarizes the center of the data without overstating precision. We did exclude country labels and focused on the crowded middle. That choice improves readability but minimizes the tails where inequality remains. We see it as acceptable only because the goal was to show a global trend and the caption disclosed what was omitted.

In the AGAINST chart, we filtered to outliers (GPI < 0.9 or > 1.1), labeled those countries, and kept the 1.0 baseline. We consider this a persuasive and deceptive technique because it spotlights exceptions and can make gaps feel more common than they are. We view our use as ethically risky but justifiable because we stated the thresholds, presented the complementary distribution, and kept a consistent baseline to avoid scale manipulation. It would become misleading if we hid how many countries were removed, changed scales to inflate gaps, or implied that these outliers represent the norm.

This project refined our view of ethical analysis and visualization to show the real distribution and context, state choices clearly, and give access to details like filtering thresholds. We believe persuasion is acceptable when you highlight a meaningful baseline, aggregate to answer a clear question, filter a defined subset, and label claims while disclosing parameters and context. It becomes misleading when choices distort magnitude, prevalence, or uncertainty, such as deceptive axes, skewed binning, or emphasis that implies frequency or causality the data do not support.
